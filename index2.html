Understanding the bsid.xls File

In this document, we familiarize ourselves with the bsid.xls file that Dr. Walker gave us.

Note that the acronym bsid translates to Bayley Scales of Infant Development. Here, we will refer to this simply as the Bayley. We refer to children in our sample who took the Bayley as Bayley Children.

Possible Outcome Variables

The Bayley tests children in four overall domains: cognitive, language, motor, and social-emotional.

For each overall domain, we could view the categorical composite score classification as the "final result" of the test.

If we want more detail (i.e., if we prefer to use a continuous variable as opposed to a categorical variable), then, instead of looking at classifications, we can view the composite score of a domain as the "final result" of the test. A child's classification for a domain is directly determined by their composite score for that domain.

Meanwhile, we can view the total raw score as the "initial result" of the test. The total raw score gets converted to a scaled score, which can then be converted to a percentile rank along with the aforementioned composite score.

Note that we can use the percentile rank measure to compare the children in our sample with children from the general population. In particular, percentile ranks correspond directly to composite score classifications. For example, we see that "Extremely Low" and "Borderline" classifications are common for our sample, but, according to their corresponding percentile ranks, they are uncommon in the general population.

The language and motor domains can be broken down into sub-domains. Sub-domains are utilized for total raw scores and scaled scores, but not for percentile ranks and composite scores. The language domain can be broken down into sub-domains of expressive and receptive communication. Meanwhile, the motor domain can be broken down into sub-domains of fine and gross motor skills.

Other Variables in the Data Set

In this section, we look at some of the other variables that were given to us in the data set.

Adjustment for Prematurity

In the Bayley hand book, we are told to "adjust for the child's prematurity through 24 months of chronological age." This is a two step process:

  • 1. Subtract the child’s date of birth from the expected date of birth. Enter the number of months and days in the row labeled Adjustment for Prematurity.
  • 2. Subtract the adjustment for prematurity from the child’s age to obtain the Adjusted Age.
The "months" and "days" from Step 1 are given to us in the data set.

Some Possible Routes to Explore

If we wish to explore this data, then there are several ways we could proceed. For example, we might want to see if the 12 month ASQ "predicts" the Bayley. But note that the Bayley has several domains, so we will eventually have to generate more specific hypotheses.

Moreover, we will have to consider the fact that, for some children, the 12 month ASQ will be their second or third exam, while, for others, the 12 month ASQ will be their first ASQ exam. Should be consider "learning effects"?
