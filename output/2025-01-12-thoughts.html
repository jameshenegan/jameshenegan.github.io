Below are some suggestions to strengthen your presentation and ensure it resonates with upper management, fits into a 12-minute window, and clearly demonstrates why this project is worth pursuing. Most of these ideas don’t require big script overhauls, just some slight restructuring or emphasis changes.

1. Provide a Strong Opening “Hook”

  • Start with the impact: In your opening, quickly highlight why GEO (Generative Engine Optimization) is urgent. For instance, you could mention, “AI-driven search results are poised to become the default method consumers use to find information, and if we don’t adapt now, we risk losing valuable visibility.”
  • Tie to business goals: Mention how increased AI visibility can translate to revenue, brand credibility, or market leadership. Upper management loves hearing about how an initiative supports the company’s larger goals.

Example Edit to Speaker 1’s Intro (optional tweak):

“Good morning. Thank you for giving us the opportunity to share a key initiative that can keep us ahead of the curve in the evolving world of AI-driven search. With Generative Search Engines quickly taking center stage, our aim today is to show you how a small but strategic investment in Generative Engine Optimization can yield a significant boost in brand visibility—and ultimately, revenue.”

2. Keep the Language Tight and High-Level

  • Eliminate repetition or overly detailed explanations: Since you have just 12 minutes and a group of four senior managers listening, try to reduce any background detail that doesn’t directly support your main argument.
  • Focus on the “so what?”: Emphasize the direct benefits and results of implementing GEO strategies—how it helps the company stand out, capture customers early, and maintain leadership.

Tip: Consider turning some of the finer points (like the step-by-step experiment details) into concise bullet points or a visual slide. That way, you can verbally highlight just the main takeaways.

3. Reinforce Key Points with Quick Examples or Data

  • Show, don’t tell: Quickly reference a short, compelling example. For instance, if you have real numbers about how often generative search has surfaced your competitor’s content vs. yours, that’s gold to mention.
  • Highlight the cost-value ratio: You’ve mentioned that the experiment cost less than two dollars. That’s a great, memorable statistic. Make sure it’s front and center when talking about ROI.

4. Anticipate Management’s Concerns

  • Address ROI and timeline: Executives usually care about when they’ll see real results. Even if it’s approximate, give them a sense of “We can implement these changes in 4–6 weeks, and expect to see results in the AI-driven search environment in 3–6 months.”
  • Risk management: You’ve started talking about sensitive data concerns. Briefly re-emphasize that your GEO approach involves only publicly available content, so there’s minimal risk of data leakage.

5. Streamline Speaker Transitions

  • Avoid repetition during transitions: Each hand-off between speakers should be as brief as possible—something like, “Now that you see the big picture, Jason will show how we proved GEO actually works.”
  • Give each speaker a distinct focus: Make it clear why each speaker is talking—Speaker 2 covers the research, Speaker 3 covers experimental setup, Speaker 4 covers findings and metrics, Speaker 5 ties it to strategy and next steps.

6. Close with a Clear “Ask” or Next Steps

  • End with a CTA (call to action): Do you need resources to expand the experiment? Approval to implement new content guidelines? Budget sign-off? Make that request explicit.
  • Highlight a vision for the future: Mention that beyond just life insurance pages, GEO can eventually be applied to other product lines or business areas.

Example lines for Speaker 5’s wrap-up:

“Next steps include scaling these tests to additional sections of our website, refining the AI-friendly content, and integrating quick feedback mechanisms for our content teams. All we need is [outline the approvals or resources needed], and we can set this in motion immediately.”

7. Time Management Tips

  • Aim for under two minutes per person: That leaves a small buffer for Q&A. If you have five speakers, that’s 10 total minutes, plus 2 minutes for quick questions or final comments.
  • Use visual aids to cut down on spoken details: If you have a slide with a chart showing “Original Site vs. AI-Friendly Site” scores, you can simply say, “As you can see, our alternate version consistently leads to higher visibility,” instead of reading each data point aloud.

8. Style Suggestions

  1. Bullet Points + Bold Keywords: Where possible, show main takeaways as bullet points and bold the keywords. Executives often scan slides or notes quickly.
  2. Confident, Value-Focused Phrasing: Phrases like “We anticipate a significant lift” or “This will strengthen our market position” land better than “We hope to see some improvement.”
  3. Avoid Over-Technical Jargon: You have done a good job explaining LLMs, RAG, etc. Keep it at the level they need to understand the value without getting lost in the weeds.

Example of a Condensed Section (if you need to shorten)

Speaker 2 could say something like:

“We discovered a recent academic paper confirming that companies can influence how AI-powered search engines cite their content. This led us to build an in-house test framework using LLMs. Our aim: see if reorganizing our public site content into a more AI-friendly format would boost our presence in AI-generated summaries. I’ll hand it over to David to outline the experiment setup.”

Shortening sections like that frees up time for the findings and the value proposition—where your core message really shines.

Final Thoughts

  • Balance Enthusiasm with Practicality: Show excitement about GEO’s potential, but also emphasize your measured approach and minimal costs.
  • Prepare for Q&A: Questions might include: “How soon do we see results?” “What budget/resources are required?” “Are there compliance or regulatory concerns?” Having short, direct answers to these will help you shine in front of the CTO and upper management.

Overall, your content is strong and highlights a genuinely innovative approach. By tightening the language, emphasizing the immediate impact, and ending with a clear ask or next steps, you’ll give upper management a compelling reason to green-light your strategy. Good luck!
