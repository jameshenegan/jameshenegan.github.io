Here’s what those three phrases usually mean when you see them on a customer‑segmentation slide:

1. Build Dimensions

What it means After the raw data have been cleaned, analysts often engineer new, higher‑level variables—“dimensions”—that capture something more meaningful than any single column on its own. These dimensions might be:

Example raw fields Dimension they become Why bother?
Number of orders, total spend, days since last purchase RFM score (Recency‑Frequency‑Monetary) Collapses multiple purchase‑history columns into one behavioral indicator.
Ten separate product‑category flags Affinity index for “Outdoor Gear” Turns sparse product flags into a single, easy‑to‑interpret preference measure.
Zip code, county, DMA Urbanicity (rural/suburban/urban) Makes location comparable across markets.

Dimensions are often scaled or normalized so that they’re “cluster‑ready,” which is why the step appears right before Feature Selection and the actual Segmentation.

2. Psychographic

What it means While demographic variables describe who customers are (age, income) and behavioral variables describe what they do (purchase frequency, channel usage), psychographic variables try to capture why they do it—their attitudes, values, lifestyle, and motivations.

Typical psychographic sources & examples:

Source Psychographic signal
Survey question: “I prefer to buy eco‑friendly products.” Environmental consciousness score
Third‑party panel data on hobbies “Outdoor enthusiast”, “DIY home‑improver” tags
Social‑media text analysis Personality traits (e.g., “openness”, “conscientiousness”)

Psychographics add emotional and motivational texture to a segment, often explaining why two customers who look identical demographically behave very differently.

3. Customer Persona

What it means Once the math groups customers into segments, marketers translate each segment into a persona—a short, story‑like archetype that makes the data actionable.

A persona usually includes:

Element Example
Name & snapshot “Eco‑Minded Millennial ‘Sophie’ (age 28, urban apartment)”
Key stats 65 % female, median income $55 k, spends $400/yr on home goods
Psychographic hook Values sustainability and minimalist design
Signature behaviors Subscribes to refill program; reads product‑impact stories
Needs & pain points Wants zero‑waste packaging; distrusts green‑washing

Personas turn cluster IDs like “Segment 3” into something people in product, design, and sales can quickly grasp and act on.

How it all fits together on the slide you saw

  1. Data Prep → Build Dimensions Combine/transform the raw fields into ~150 richer variables.
  2. Data Analysis → keep the dimensions that matter (demographic, geographic, psychographic, behavioral).
  3. Segmentation → run clustering on 75 of those variables.
  4. Customer Personas → narrate each resulting cluster so human stakeholders can remember and use them.

One‑minute narrative of the whole workflow

  1. Data Collection Bring in the raw material. * • 1st‑party data* (your CRM, website, purchase logs) + 3rd‑party data (panels, census, lifestyle feeds).

  2. Data Preparation Make the data usable. ‑ Run quality checks (missing values, outliers) → correct or flag. ‑ Correlation & exploratory analysis to understand relationships, spot quirks. ‑ Build dimensions—engineer richer metrics (RFM scores, lifestyle indices, urbanicity, etc.). ‑ Feature selection—keep the dimensions that matter for the business, trimming thousands of columns down to ~150.

  3. Data Analysis (Feature Refinement) Frame the problem in business language. The 150 “keeper” variables fall into four intuitive buckets: Demographic, Geographic, Psychographic, and Behavioral.

  4. Segmentation Let the math find natural customer groupings. Cluster on ~75 of the highest‑signal variables, iterating until clusters are distinct, stable, and explainable.

  5. Customer Personas Translate clusters into human stories. Give each segment a name, snapshot, key stats, motivations, and signature behaviors so marketing, product, and sales teams can act on them.

Single‑slide design ideas

Layout idea Visual device Tips for keeping it readable
Left‑to‑right funnel Five trapezoids that narrow from “Data Collection” to “Personas”. Each stage gets an icon (database → wrench → filter → cluster nodes → user avatar). Use a single accent color that darkens as you move right to convey focus. Put the stage title inside the shape and 1‑line bullet(s) under it.
Vertical timeline A tall arrow or roadway divided into five stops. Number each stop (①‑⑤) and keep bullets ≤ 8 words. Put a mini‑heat‑map bar under “Data Analysis” to hint at the variable reduction (2000 → 150 → 75).
Wheel / Hub‑and‑spoke Central hub = “Customer Segmentation”; five spokes labeled with the stages. Color‑code the four variable “Areas” and reuse those colors on spoke labels so the audience sees continuity.
Swim‑lane storyboard Five horizontal lanes across the slide; each lane has an icon, a bold stage name, and a micro‑summary. Great if you want equal visual weight for each step. Keep font ≥ 18 pt and reserve the far right for a small persona card mock‑up.
Stacked blocks with KPI callouts Blocks stacked downward, each 15–20 % of slide height. Inside each block: stage name on the left, a big numeric KPI on the right (e.g., “→ 150 vars”). Works well when you want the audience to remember the numbers (2000 → 150 → 75). Use contrasting text color for the KPIs.

General slide hygiene

  • Aim for ≤ 35 words total on the slide; narrate the rest.
  • One consistent icon set keeps it professional (e.g., Feather, Font Awesome).
  • Animate only the block fill or arrow highlight as you talk through each step—less distraction, more signal.
