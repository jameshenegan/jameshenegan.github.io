ML Life Cycle

The Machine Learning (ML) life cycle is a cyclical process designed to align business goals with ML models that deliver value. It consists of three primary stages: discovery, development, and deployment, with steps that are often revisited iteratively.

1. Discovery Stage:

  • Defining the Business Use Case: Business leaders and ML practitioners collaborate to understand the business problem and assess the feasibility of using ML to solve it. A clear definition of goals and success metrics (KPIs) is established, and the business value of ML is continually considered.
  • Data Exploration: Data quality and availability are evaluated through visualization and statistical techniques to ensure that relevant, high-quality data is available. Data stewards help determine if additional data is required or if data privacy issues exist.
  • Feasibility Study: A short technical sprint explores ML model options, framing, and data quality to assess the viability of solving the business problem.

2. Development Stage:

  • Data Pipelines and Feature Engineering: Data pipelines are built to clean and process raw data for ML models. Feature engineering transforms raw data into model-usable formats, aligning with the learning objective.
  • Model Building: Multiple models are trained and validated on prepared data. Model performance is measured using predetermined evaluation metrics from the discovery stage. Hyperparameters are tuned, and reproducibility is ensured through best practices.
  • Result Presentation: Key insights and model performance are communicated to stakeholders to assess whether to move forward with deployment.

3. Deployment Stage:

  • Planning for Deployment: After successful development, the model is prepared for production. Decisions related to model retraining, data handling, inference requirements, and latency issues are addressed.
  • Operationalization (MLOps): Automation, monitoring, and maintenance of the model in production are crucial. Continuous integration/delivery (CI/CD) pipelines ensure efficient model updates and reliability.
  • Monitoring and Maintenance: Once deployed, models are monitored for staleness, data drift, and prediction quality. This includes tracking feature and label distributions and external data dependencies like APIs.
