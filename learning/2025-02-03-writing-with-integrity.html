It can be inspiring—and deeply motivating—to view the act of writing anything, even “dry” company documentation, in the larger context of truth, integrity, and service. If you want to bring a sense of reverence to your documentation, consider these perspectives:

  1. Documentation as an Extension of Truth-Telling

    • In the spirit of Jordan Peterson’s emphasis on not lying—or speaking with integrity in The Four Agreements—you can treat your documentation as a site where clarity and honesty truly matter.
    • Every sentence in your documentation becomes an exercise in saying exactly what you mean. Instead of padding the text with jargon or uncertain statements, aim for precision. This doesn’t just improve the doc’s quality; it also cultivates your own habit of careful truth-telling.

  2. Connecting to the Larger “Logos”

    • Historically, writing was a laborious act. People wrote on papyrus scrolls, clay tablets, or by carving letters into stone. The very effort involved meant they did not record what was trivial.
    • Even though we now have the luxury of typing in a word processor (or even dictating into a phone), you can choose to approach documentation with that same mindfulness. Do a mental check: “If I had to chisel this into stone, would I still include it?”
    • This practice will push you to prioritize what’s most essential and communicate it effectively, treating your words as an extension of the sacred tradition of “putting truth into text.”

  3. Service and Empathy

    • One reason religious or philosophical texts feel “important” is that they aim to serve humanity at large—to teach, to guide, to heal.
    • Documentation is also a form of service: it helps colleagues, new hires, or even future versions of yourself. In a sense, you’re teaching and guiding, ensuring that knowledge isn’t lost or misunderstood.
    • If you approach your writing as an act of service—like a teacher creating a clear textbook—it becomes more than a chore. It becomes a meaningful contribution to the collective knowledge base of your team.

  4. Quality Over Quantity

    • Our time is flooded with text—emails, social media posts, memos. Because it’s so easy to write, we can forget the importance of thoughtful composition.
    • Adopting a mindset of deliberate creation helps ensure you’re producing something of lasting value. Ask yourself: “What’s the most concise but most accurate way to convey this information?” This not only reduces clutter, but also demonstrates respect for your readers’ time.

  5. Craftsmanship and Pride

    • Think of your documentation as craftsmanship. Even if it isn’t destined for the public realm like a best-selling philosophy book, you can still have pride in your craft.
    • Pay attention to structure, clarity, and style. A well-crafted piece of documentation—logical headings, clear examples, coherent flow—elevates the text from “just another file” to something that people turn to as an authoritative, user-friendly reference.

  6. Longevity and Legacy

    • It might not be the Bible or Plato’s dialogues, but your documentation could be used or referenced for years to come, long after you’ve moved on from that role.
    • Think about a future person who might rely on your words to solve a critical problem or avoid a major pitfall. In that sense, there’s a real “historical” quality to even the simplest documentation.
    • Taking care in your writing is a way of showing respect to those future readers. You are, in a small but meaningful way, shaping the future of your organization’s knowledge.

  7. Staying Grounded in Purpose

    • You can hold both truths at once: your documentation might not save mankind, but it certainly can make life and work easier for those around you.
    • That alone is a purpose worth respecting. If you see documentation as a form of stewardship—helping preserve and transmit what’s valuable—you can imbue it with the spirit of doing good work for the benefit of others.

Practical Tips to Bring This Perspective into Your Writing

  • Outline with Intention: Before writing, outline your topics and ask, “Why is each piece of information important? What question does it answer?” This keeps you from adding superfluous text.
  • Use Clear Language: Strive for clarity and precision. Eliminate unnecessary jargon. If you had to read your own documentation in a hurry under stress, would it be easy to understand?
  • Incorporate Examples: Well-chosen examples help readers internalize information. This is reminiscent of parables or stories in ancient texts—it shows, rather than tells.
  • Iterate: Like any craft, good writing benefits from revision. Draft, step away, and come back with fresh eyes to see if every sentence is necessary, honest, and clear.
  • Invite Feedback: Documentation is a living text that serves others. Ask peers for feedback, just as teachers and philosophers relied on dialogue. This helps refine and ensure your words serve the intended purpose.

In essence, you can honor the “power of the word” by creating documentation that is accurate, thoughtful, and genuinely useful. You may not be shaping Western philosophy, but you are providing knowledge, clarity, and continuity within your company—and that is a worthy undertaking in its own right. By treating your words with care and aligning your writing with a spirit of integrity and service, you’ll elevate even seemingly mundane tasks into something more meaningful and enduring.
