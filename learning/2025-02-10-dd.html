Saturday Morning

1. Refine the Problem Statement and Scope

  • Meet with your manager(s) this week to confirm what “success” looks like at the end of the 10-week project.
  • Identify the key questions you need answered before moving forward (e.g., “Do we need specific tool recommendations, or just a prototype?” “What’s the exact definition of ‘standardization’ for our department?”).
  • Based on that conversation, finalize the MVP deliverables: for example, a clearly defined list of required documentation items and a handful of completed examples in a consistent, standardized format.

2. Solidify a Documentation Framework

  • Categorize documentation into a few main types (e.g., process vs. technical vs. standards). You’ve already started this, but try to finalize your list of categories so each team can see where their work fits.
  • Draft a simple template for each type of documentation. At minimum, include headings like Purpose, Scope, Steps (for processes), Technical Details, and any relevant references.

Example Template Layout

  1. Title: Clear name/identifier
  2. Purpose: Why this document is needed
  3. Scope: Which teams/systems/processes it covers
  4. Details: Step-by-step instructions for processes, or architectural overviews for technical docs
  5. Ownership: Who is responsible for updates?
  6. Revision History: Who changed what, and when

Even a one-page template helps make documentation consistent and easier to scan.

3. Pilot a Few Tools

  • Shortlist the tools your team might realistically use. For instance, if the organization is heavily into Microsoft products, SharePoint and Word templates might be simplest. If there is flexibility, you could consider Confluence, Notion, or GitBook.
  • Test out each tool (if possible) by creating a small sample document in each. Look at ease of use, searchability, version control, and how it handles multimedia (screenshots, screen recordings, etc.).
  • Present tool pros/cons to your manager or the team. This helps leadership see what might be involved in adoption and licensing.

4. Gather Content From Each Team

  • Have each of the four teams (Data Analytics, Data Engineering, Business Intelligence, and Data Governance) list their top 3–5 processes or systems that they consider high priority for documentation.
  • Encourage each team to pick one example and fill out the new documentation template. This will serve as your MVP “showcase.”
  • Focus on quality over quantity—if people see a solid format, they’ll be more likely to follow it.

5. Implement Naming Conventions and Version Control

  • Propose naming conventions for files and folders, including consistent prefixes, dates, or version tags, to make them easy to locate.
  • Decide on a basic version control approach—maybe just a “v1.0, v1.1” system or something more sophisticated if you use a platform like Git or a built-in Confluence feature.
  • Make sure each team knows the naming rules and where the “master copy” of each document resides.

6. Provide Guidance & Training

  • Offer short walkthroughs or training sessions for each team on how to use the chosen tool(s) and follow the documentation template.
  • If you want to incorporate screen recordings and AI-based transcripts, demonstrate how it might help them capture knowledge quickly and then edit for clarity.

7. Review & Iterate

  • Set up a feedback loop—after teams produce their sample documentation, gather feedback on the template, the process, and the tools.
  • Refine the standards as needed, and make sure you capture everyone’s suggestions.
  • As you head into the final weeks, compile all your results and learnings into a project summary or short final presentation for leadership.

8. Plan Beyond the MVP

  • After the 10-week period, you’ll still have until year-end to actually “clean up” all documentation. Draft a post-MVP roadmap that outlines next steps, including:
    • Rolling the template out to other teams or departments.
    • Establishing ongoing maintenance/updates.
    • Possibly integrating more advanced automation or AI-based solutions (like automated transcription or generative content drafting) once you have a stable framework in place.

Key Takeaway:

  • Keep it simple at first. A short, workable template plus a clear, centralized location is often enough to demonstrate value.
  • Involve each team to make sure you capture their most critical processes or systems.
  • Document everything you learn so you can hand off a sustainable approach to the rest of the department and future collaborators.

By taking these steps, your team will not only meet the short-term MVP goal but also lay the foundation for a long-term, efficient documentation culture within your department.

Sunday Morning

The Data Docs Project: Introduction

The Data Docs Project is a department-wide initiative involving members from each team in Corporate Data and Analytics—Business Intelligence, Data Governance, Data Engineering, and Data Analytics. The project focuses on how we create, store, and access documentation for the various processes we manage as a department.

Main Objectives

  1. Centralization of Documentation
    Currently, our knowledge is scattered across multiple platforms: Azure DevOps boards, Word documents, Excel files on SharePoint, emails, chat messages, or even in people’s heads. Our first objective is to identify and consolidate the most critical information into a single, easily accessible location.

  2. Standardization of Documentation
    While each team’s processes differ, many share common elements. We aim to develop a consistent, readable structure that highlights these similarities—without forcing every process into an ill-fitting template. We should not alter established business practices just to fit a “one-size-fits-all” format. Instead, we need a flexible but uniform approach that promotes clarity and usability while accommodating each process’s unique aspects.

  3. Ease of Use
    Both writing and finding documentation should be simple and straightforward. A user-friendly documentation solution increases the likelihood that team members will:

    • Keep the documentation up to date.
    • Find relevant information quickly.
    • Understand the broader context of projects and initiatives.
    • Drill down into additional details when needed.

    Our solution should be searchable, easy to digest, and designed to encourage collaboration and continuous improvement.

Why Documentation Matters

  1. Clarity and Alignment
    Good documentation ensures that teams share the same understanding of goals, requirements, and processes. It reduces confusion, repetitive questions, and reliance on unspoken “tribal” knowledge.

  2. Efficiency and Scalability
    Well-documented procedures streamline onboarding and troubleshooting. We often pose the scenario, “If a team member suddenly leaves—maybe they win the lottery—could someone else step in and carry out their processes?” Proper documentation helps make that possible.

  3. Professionalism and Consistency
    A standardized style and structure demonstrate a cohesive brand identity. Clear formatting also reduces the cognitive burden for people who read or skim multiple documents.

Foundations of Good Documentation

  1. Well-Defined Audience and Scope
    Tailor content to the readers’ roles and technical backgrounds. Clearly define what is included and what is out of scope.

  2. Clarity and Conciseness
    Favor plain language, avoid unnecessary jargon, and define acronyms.

  3. Structure and Formatting
    Use consistent headings, page layouts, numbering, and version control. Incorporate visuals (figures, tables, diagrams) to illustrate complex ideas. Maintain a style guide that details fonts, spacing, labeling conventions, and corporate branding guidelines.

  4. Maintenance and Revision
    Documentation must evolve with each product or process. Assign owners, automate reminders, and schedule regular reviews to keep content fresh.

Types of Documentation

We’ve recognized that not all documentation is the same. Some common categories include:

  1. Business Process Documentation
    Outlines all the actions a person must perform to complete a task or carry out a process. For example, a step-by-step guide that walks a user through a recurring workflow.

  2. Technical Documentation
    Explains how automated systems or software operate, enabling others to debug or modify them as needed. This is especially important for data pipelines, machine learning processes, and other automated tasks.

  3. Project Management Documentation
    Includes meeting agendas and minutes, scope statements, and decision logs—essential for tracking project progress and decisions.

  4. Standards or Naming Conventions
    Details how to name databases, views, tables, and other objects consistently. This might include guidelines like camelCase, snake_case, or prefix conventions for different types of data assets.

  5. High-Level Goals or Mission Statements
    Broad statements that ensure everyone understands the overall objectives and direction of the department or project.

Deciding What to Document

In one discussion, we focused on identifying which processes and items actually need documentation. We agreed on the importance of documenting any regular, repeatable processes. We also debated whether to document every one-off project. The consensus was that we don’t need to document trivial or purely unique events. However, if a one-off project involves a unique or complex issue, what was learned should be documented to prevent “reinventing the wheel” later.

We also acknowledged some confusion around terms like “processes,” “procedures,” or “workflows,” but agreed they all refer to how we consistently get things done. For clarity, we may just pick one term (e.g., “process”) and use it consistently.

Tools and Platforms

A large part of our conversation covered potential tools and platforms for creating and organizing documentation. Examples include:

  • Obsidian
  • Confluence
  • Notion
  • SharePoint (potentially with Word documents stored in a central library)

We discussed the possibility of using screen recordings and then transcribing them into text for searching and summarizing. This would make it easy to add quick how-to videos alongside written explanations.

However, we also recognized that the real challenge is writing good documentation, not just picking a tool. Tools can help, but the effort to document procedures accurately still requires human input and maintenance.

Format and Consistency

We want a balance between consistency and flexibility. Different document types—process docs, project logs, technical references—may not look identical, but they should share certain standard elements where relevant, such as:

  • Introduction or Summary
  • Purpose
  • Stakeholders
  • Inputs and Outputs
  • Permissions or Security Requirements
  • Version/Revision History

When these elements do appear, they should be presented in a recognizable way across teams.

Naming Conventions and Storage

We need to decide where documents are stored and how they are named to avoid confusion. This involves setting up:

  • A clear folder or space structure (e.g., SharePoint site, Confluence space).
  • A consistent naming scheme for files (potentially including version numbers and dates).

We also talked about examples where a data architect provided strict naming conventions for databases and views, such as guidelines for base views, selection views, derived views, interface views, and associations. That kind of documentation is essential for consistency in data engineering work.

Resistance and Job Security Concerns

We acknowledged that some individuals might worry that sharing detailed documentation threatens their job security. It’s important to emphasize that thorough documentation is about knowledge sharing and team continuity, not replacing roles.

Enhancing Documentation with Video

Videos can be helpful for tasks that are easier to demonstrate visually. Screen recordings, especially when paired with transcripts, allow for more engaging and accessible guides. The text from these transcripts can be searched or summarized by various tools, making the overall process more efficient.

Version Control and Updates

Documentation becomes outdated quickly if it’s not actively maintained. We need a clear version control process, which might be as simple as:

  • Keeping a version table in Word documents (last updated date, who updated, summary of changes).
  • Using a platform that supports version control (like Git or built-in Confluence features).

Regular reviews or scheduled reminders ensure content stays relevant.

Examples from Data Engineering

In Data Engineering, a typical example might be documenting a data flow from source systems into the enterprise data warehouse. This would include:

  • Where the source tables live.
  • What transformations are performed.
  • How the data flows through staging, integration, and presentation layers.

Such technical details are crucial for troubleshooting and onboarding new team members.

In summary, the Data Docs Project aims to establish a cohesive, department-wide approach to documentation that is centralized, standardized, and user-friendly. By defining different types of documentation, clarifying which processes to document, exploring tools, and creating consistent formats and naming conventions, we hope to improve knowledge sharing, efficiency, and alignment across all teams in Corporate Data and Analytics.

Notion, Confluence, Obsidian, etc.

Notion, Confluence, and Obsidian are all potential platforms you could use for creating, organizing, and sharing data documentation. Each one has different strengths and trade-offs, so your choice may depend on your department’s collaboration style, technical requirements, and existing company infrastructure. Here’s a quick overview of how each tool might fit into your Data Docs Project:

1. Notion

  • All-in-One Workspace
    Notion provides an intuitive blend of document creation, wikis, databases, and project management features. This flexibility can help you unify process docs, technical references, meeting notes, and more—all in one platform.
  • Ease of Collaboration
    Teams can edit pages in real time, leave comments, and track changes. Notion’s built-in templates and AI features (for writing, summarizing, and searching) can speed up documentation tasks.
  • Customizable Yet Simple
    While Notion is highly customizable, it remains relatively user-friendly, which can reduce onboarding friction. The downside is that with so many features, some teams might need guidance to avoid confusion or “over-engineering” their workspace.
  • Potential Fit for the Data Docs Project
    If you need a collaborative, visually appealing way to store a variety of documentation types (processes, technical specs, etc.) and quickly adapt as the project evolves, Notion is a strong contender.

2. Confluence

  • Enterprise Wiki Structure
    Confluence is well-known for organizing corporate knowledge. Its “Spaces” and “Pages” model fosters a clear hierarchy, making it easy to structure department-wide documentation.
  • Atlassian Ecosystem
    It integrates seamlessly with Jira and other Atlassian products—great if your teams already use Jira for project tracking. Confluence supports collaborative editing, comments, and version history for your pages.
  • Governance and Scalability
    Confluence has robust permission settings and can handle large user bases, which is beneficial if your life insurance company’s documentation initiative spans multiple departments.
  • Potential Fit for the Data Docs Project
    If your organization values a more traditional wiki approach, is already invested in the Atlassian ecosystem, or needs enterprise-level governance, Confluence is a safe and scalable choice.

3. Obsidian

  • Local-First, Markdown-Based
    Obsidian stores all notes locally as plain Markdown files. This can be advantageous if your team needs to retain control over data, avoid proprietary lock-in, or work offline.
  • Backlinks and Graph View
    It excels at creating “knowledge maps” through internal linking and a graph view, which can be great for visually exploring how different processes or technical components connect.
  • Highly Customizable & Lightweight
    Obsidian’s plugin ecosystem and theming options offer plenty of flexibility. However, it is more of a note-taking/personal knowledge management tool than a traditional collaborative wiki, so team collaboration might require additional setup (e.g., using a shared drive or Obsidian Sync).
  • Potential Fit for the Data Docs Project
    If you’re dealing with a smaller, tech-savvy team that values offline access, deep linking between concepts, and custom workflows—or if your documentation approach is more developer-oriented—Obsidian might be ideal. For broad, department-wide adoption, you’d need to ensure you have a solid syncing/collaboration strategy in place.

Which Tool Is Best for Data Documentation?

It ultimately depends on your department’s collaboration style, technical requirements, IT constraints, and future maintenance plans. If you want an enterprise-grade wiki closely tied to project management, Confluence might fit. If you need a highly flexible, visually appealing workspace that can handle everything from process docs to databases in a single place, Notion is appealing. If your priority is local data ownership, advanced linking, and a developer-friendly environment, Obsidian stands out.

In many organizations, SharePoint (already part of Microsoft 365) or a combination of these tools might also make sense—especially if the company wants to minimize new licenses. The key is to test a small sample of documents in each tool, gather feedback from team members, and see how well each platform meets your documentation goals of being centralized, standardized, and easy to use.
