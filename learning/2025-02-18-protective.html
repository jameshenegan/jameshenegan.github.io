This passage discusses the fundamental concept behind a protective value study, which is an evaluation used in underwriting (typically in insurance) to determine whether a particular underwriting procedure is economically justified. The core idea is straightforward: a procedure is considered valuable if the cost of implementing it is less than the savings it generates by reducing mortality (for life insurance) or morbidity (for health insurance).

Key Points:

  1. Economic Justification: The underwriting procedure must save more money (via reduced mortality/morbidity) than it costs to implement.
  2. Core Question: “Will I save more than I spend?” This is the essence of protective value analysis.
  3. Challenges: While the question is simple, determining the answer is often complex due to the methods involved.
  4. Purpose of the Paper: To introduce a simple and general model that can be used to conduct protective value studies.

This passage explains how insurance pricing and underwriting procedures are connected to risk assessment.

  1. Standard vs. Substandard Risk:

    • Insurance prices are set based on an assumed “standard” level of mortality (for life insurance) or morbidity (for health insurance).
    • If an applicant has a higher risk than the standard level, they should ideally be charged more or denied coverage.

  2. Purpose of Underwriting Procedures:

    • An underwriting procedure is valuable if it can identify applicants with higher-than-standard risk (substandard risk).
    • Without such a procedure, insurers would mistakenly charge standard rates to higher-risk individuals, leading to financial losses.

  3. Key to Protective Value:

    • The effectiveness of an underwriting procedure depends on how well it detects excess mortality/morbidity.
    • The better the procedure is at identifying high-risk individuals, the greater the financial savings for the insurer.

Takeaway:

The protective value of an underwriting procedure is determined by its ability to prevent insurers from underpricing high-risk individuals. If a procedure can accurately detect those with above-average mortality/morbidity, it helps insurers charge appropriately, leading to financial savings.

This passage explains how excess mortality/morbidity (M/M) is measured in underwriting and how different underwriting decisions impact this measurement.

Key Points:

  1. Measuring Excess Mortality/Morbidity:

    • The most common method is to express substandard M/M as a percentage of standard M/M (e.g., 150% means 50% higher risk than standard).
    • This method aligns with reinsurance manuals, which insurers refer to when assessing medical impairments.

  2. Handling Special Cases:

    • Declined Applications: When an applicant is declined, their M/M may be estimated as the maximum rating an insurer would issue + 50% extra.
    • Not Taken Applications (Not Takens):
      • If the applicant was offered standard coverage but declined, they are assumed to have standard M/M.
      • If the applicant was offered substandard coverage but declined, they are assumed to have substandard M/M.
    • Exclusions & Plan Modifications:
      • When an insurer modifies the coverage (e.g., excluding a condition), it’s trickier to assess M/M.
      • If no substandard rating is given alongside the exclusion, it might be reasonable to assume the exclusion equals the highest acceptable substandard rating.

Takeaway:

Excess M/M is usually measured as a percentage above the standard level. Special cases like declined policies, not-taken offers, and exclusions require adjustments to accurately estimate excess risk. The goal is to ensure fair pricing and accurate risk assessment in underwriting.

This passage highlights the role of persistency (the percentage of policyholders who keep their insurance policies over time) in evaluating the savings impact of an underwriting procedure.

Key Points:

  1. Definition of Persistency:

    • High persistency means low lapse rates (fewer policyholders canceling their policies).
    • Low persistency means high lapse rates (more policyholders dropping their coverage).

  2. Impact on Excess Mortality/Morbidity (M/M):

    • When persistency is high, more policyholders continue coverage for a longer time.
    • This increases the number of insured individuals, leading to a higher total excess M/M (because more people remain covered, including higher-risk individuals).

  3. Relevance to Savings:

    • Since underwriting procedures aim to detect and price risk correctly, persistency affects the long-term cost savings of these procedures.
    • If more high-risk individuals stay insured, the importance of accurately assessing their risk increases.

Takeaway:

Persistency directly influences the effectiveness of underwriting. When more policyholders keep their policies for longer, the cumulative impact of excess mortality/morbidity grows, making accurate risk assessment even more critical for maximizing savings.

This passage discusses a second reason why persistency is important, particularly for substandard policyholders.

Key Points:

  1. Substandard Policyholders Have Fewer Options:

    • People with higher health risks (substandard insureds) often accept substandard coverage only because they have no better alternative.
    • Unlike standard insureds, they cannot easily switch to a cheaper or better policy elsewhere.

  2. Higher Persistency Among Substandard Policyholders:

    • Because they lack alternative options, substandard policyholders tend to keep their policies longer than standard insureds.
    • This leads to higher persistency in the substandard risk group.

  3. Why Substandard Policyholders Avoid Lapsing:

    • If a substandard insured voluntarily cancels (lapses) their policy, they often know that:
      • They might not qualify for new coverage anywhere else.
      • If they do qualify, it may be even more expensive than their current policy.

Takeaway:

Persistency is especially high among substandard insureds because they have limited alternatives. They are less likely to cancel their policies, knowing that obtaining new coverage could be difficult or impossible. This makes accurate risk assessment even more critical for insurers, as substandard policyholders are likely to stay on the books longer.

This passage explains the importance of the interest rate when evaluating the savings from an underwriting procedure.

Key Points:

  1. Excess Mortality/Morbidity (M/M) Occurs Over Time:

    • The financial impact of excess M/M is spread over many years, potentially for the entire life of an insurance contract.
    • To properly assess the value of an underwriting procedure, insurers must convert future excess M/M savings into present value.

  2. Present Value Calculation:

    • Since costs (such as the expense of an underwriting procedure) occur immediately, while the savings (from avoided excess M/M) occur gradually, a present value comparison is needed.
    • This allows insurers to directly compare the up-front cost of underwriting vs. the long-term savings.

  3. Impact of Interest Rate:

    • The chosen interest rate reflects the insurer’s time-value of money (i.e., how much they value money now versus later).
    • Higher interest rates → Lower present value of excess M/M savings (because future savings are discounted more).
    • Lower interest rates → Higher present value of excess M/M savings (because future savings are discounted less).

Takeaway:

Since underwriting savings happen over time, insurers discount future savings to present value using an interest rate. The higher the rate, the lower the present value of future savings, meaning the underwriting procedure might appear less beneficial. The chosen rate should reflect the insurer’s financial outlook and time-value preferences.

This passage explains how prevalence (the proportion of the insured population that actually has excess mortality/morbidity (M/M)) affects the overall savings from an underwriting procedure.

Key Points:

  1. Accuracy Alone Isn’t Enough:

    • Even if an underwriting procedure is perfectly accurate (no false positives or false negatives), its overall financial impact depends on how common excess M/M is in the insured population.
    • If very few people have excess M/M, even a perfect test won’t lead to significant savings.

  2. The Role of Prevalence:

    • The true proportion of people with excess M/M influences the value of an underwriting procedure.
    • If prevalence is high, identifying excess M/M can lead to substantial savings.
    • If prevalence is low, the potential for savings is smaller, even if the procedure works perfectly.

  3. Impact on Underwriting Strategy:

    • When prevalence is high, stricter underwriting procedures may be more justified.
    • When prevalence is low, costly or invasive procedures may not be worth the expense.

Takeaway:

Even a perfect underwriting procedure will only generate meaningful savings if a significant portion of the insured population actually has excess M/M. Understanding prevalence helps insurers decide which procedures are worth using based on their potential financial impact.

  3. Age-Based Variations in Prevalence:

    • Some conditions, like coronary artery disease, become more common as people age.
    • This means underwriting requirements may depend on both age and the amount of insurance requested.
    • Example: Older applicants may face stricter health screenings for heart disease.

Takeaway:

Insurers adjust their underwriting procedures based on prevalence trends to balance cost-effectiveness and risk assessment. They may limit testing to certain policy sizes, adjust requirements based on location, or increase screening for older applicants based on disease patterns.

This passage highlights a commonly overlooked factor in protective value studies: the cost of undetected excess mortality/morbidity (M/M) when not all applicants undergo underwriting procedures.

Key Points:

  1. Not Testing Everyone Leads to Undetected Risk:

    • To save costs, underwriting procedures are often performed only on applicants seeking higher insurance amounts.
    • However, this means some applicants with excess M/M (who apply for lower amounts) go undetected, leading to potential financial losses for the insurer.

  2. The Trade-off Between Testing Costs & Undetected M/M:

    • The decision to limit testing is usually based on prevalence—if a condition is rare, testing everyone isn’t cost-effective.
    • But not testing everyone comes with a cost: some high-risk applicants will slip through and be charged standard rates, leading to underpricing and potential losses.

  3. Using Business Distribution to Estimate Undetected Risk:

    • If insurers know how their policies are distributed by coverage amount, they can estimate how much undetected M/M is costing them.
    • This helps determine whether a broader or more selective underwriting approach is more financially beneficial.

Takeaway:

Limiting underwriting to higher-value policies saves on testing costs but risks missing high-risk individuals who take smaller policies. The cost of undetected M/M should be factored into the overall evaluation of an underwriting procedure to ensure a balanced approach between cost savings and risk management.

This passage introduces the decision criteria used to determine whether an underwriting procedure is financially justified.

Key Points:

  1. Core Question:

    • The fundamental decision is: “Will I save more than I spend?”
    • To answer this, insurers use two primary methods.

  2. Two Decision Criteria:

    • Break-even Method:
      • This method determines whether the savings from the procedure at least equal its cost.
      • If savings ≥ cost, the procedure is considered worth implementing.
    • Return on Investment (ROI) Method:
      • This method evaluates how much return is generated per dollar spent on the procedure.
      • ROI helps compare different procedures or investment options by measuring profitability.

  3. Differences Between the Two:

    • The Break-even Method is a pass/fail test: does the procedure cover its cost or not?
    • The ROI Method provides a more detailed financial perspective, showing how efficient the investment is.

Takeaway:

Insurers use Break-even Analysis to check if a procedure is viable and ROI Analysis to assess its profitability. Understanding both methods helps in making better underwriting decisions.

This passage explains the Break-even Method, a way to determine if an underwriting procedure is financially justified.

Key Points:

  1. Definition of Break-even:

    • The break-even (or threshold) level is the amount of insurance at which the savings from the underwriting procedure exactly equal its cost.
    • If an applicant’s insurance amount is above this threshold, the procedure is cost-effective.

  2. Break-even Amount May Not Be Unique:

    • There can be multiple break-even points, meaning different insurance amounts might yield the same financial result.
    • The example mentioned (but not provided) would demonstrate situations where different levels of coverage still result in a break-even outcome.

  3. Interest Rate Considerations:

    • The interest rate used in break-even calculations is arbitrary but fixed.
    • However, it should reflect the insurer’s time-value of money, meaning how the company values money now vs. in the future.
    • A reasonable choice for this rate is the one the insurer uses to establish policy reserves (the money set aside to pay future claims).

Takeaway:

The Break-even Method helps determine the minimum amount of insurance needed for an underwriting procedure to be cost-effective. It relies on fixed interest rates and may have multiple break-even points. The chosen interest rate should align with the insurer’s financial strategy, such as policy reserve calculations.

Summary & Explanation:

This passage explains the Return on Investment (ROI) Method, another way to assess whether an underwriting procedure is financially beneficial.

Key Points:

  1. How ROI is Calculated:

    • The ROI method finds the interest rate at which the savings from the underwriting procedure exactly equal its cost for a given amount of insurance.
    • This interest rate is referred to as the ROI.

  2. Decision Rule:

    • If the ROI is greater than the insurer’s time-value of money (i.e., the rate the insurer uses for financial decision-making), then the procedure has protective value.
    • This means the insurer is earning a return on the underwriting procedure that is higher than their internal financial benchmark, making the procedure a worthwhile investment.

  3. Comparison to the Break-even Method:

    • If the ROI equals the insurer’s time-value of money, the ROI method and the Break-even method will yield the same result.
    • The Break-even method finds the insurance amount where savings = cost, while the ROI method finds the interest rate where savings = cost for a given insurance amount.

Takeaway:

The ROI Method evaluates the profitability of an underwriting procedure by determining whether its return exceeds the insurer’s financial benchmark. If ROI > time-value of money, the procedure is beneficial. If ROI = time-value of money, it provides the same insights as the Break-even Method.
