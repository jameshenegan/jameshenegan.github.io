Protective Value

Detailed Summary

This paper introduces the concept of a protective value study for underwriting procedures in insurance. The essential question is whether a particular underwriting process saves more (through identifying and appropriately charging for higher mortality or morbidity risks) than it costs to implement. The author lays out a straightforward but thorough framework for conducting such an analysis, focusing on the following key elements:

1. Fundamental Question

  • Core Principle: An underwriting procedure has positive economic value if the present value of savings in mortality/morbidity (M/M) exceeds the total cost of performing the procedure.
  • The savings arise because, without the procedure, an insurer would charge standard premiums to applicants who actually present substandard risks.

2. Measuring Mortality/Morbidity Savings

  1. Excess M/M:

    • Typically expressed as a percentage above the standard M/M assumption.
    • For example, if a substandard risk is projected to have 150% of the standard mortality rate, the excess 50% is what contributes to the protective value when identified.

  2. Special Considerations:

    • Declined Applications: Can be modeled as having a very high “virtual” substandard rating (e.g., standard + 50% extra).
    • Not Takens: If a substandard offer is made but not accepted, it can be viewed differently than a policy that is accepted.
    • Exclusions or Modified Coverage: If coverage modifications do not include a substandard premium rating, one might still treat them like a high substandard rating for analysis.

  3. Clinical/Alternate Data:

    • If standard reinsurance manuals or standard tables do not exist for certain impairments, the underwriter can rely on clinical or medical studies.
    • These data must be adapted (or “mapped”) to known standard tables (e.g., SOA 1975–80 Basic Mortality) to estimate the relative excess M/M.

3. Persistency

  • Impact on Savings: Higher persistency (lower lapse rate) means more individuals remain in force over time, which increases the insurer’s exposure but also increases the aggregate excess M/M collected from substandard risks.
  • Substandard Cohorts Often Have Higher Persistency: Applicants with fewer market options for coverage (due to substandard health) may be less likely to lapse. This elevates their overall contribution to excess M/M.

4. Interest Rate and Time Period

  1. Discounting Future Savings:

    • Because excess M/M savings occur over future years, they must be discounted to a present value.
    • A higher discount rate reduces the present value of those future M/M savings; a lower rate increases it.

  2. Time Horizon:

    • The number of years included in the analysis will affect total calculated savings.
    • Over many years, especially if a policy can remain in force for a long duration, the cumulative M/M differences can be significant.
    • Choosing a shorter-than-maximum horizon understates the potential savings.

5. Prevalence

  • Definition: The proportion of applicants who actually have the excess mortality/morbidity condition.

  • Influence on Protective Value:

    1. Low Prevalence: It may be inefficient to test everyone if very few applicants exhibit the impairment. Thus, insurers often raise the coverage thresholds or target specific ages and amounts for more thorough underwriting.
    2. Geographical Variation: Different regions can have different prevalence rates (e.g., HIV infection rates). This can drive selective use of certain tests in certain territories (subject to legal constraints).
    3. Age Variation: Some conditions, such as coronary artery disease, occur more frequently at older ages, guiding insurers to introduce underwriting requirements by age bands.

  • Partial Testing and “Untested” Risk: If an insurer chooses not to test all applicants (e.g., below a certain coverage amount), some proportion of substandard risks will remain unidentified. The associated “missed” excess M/M is a cost that must be accounted for.

6. Costs of the Procedure

  • All-In Costs:
    • Direct cost of the test or report (e.g., physician’s statement, lab test).
    • Ancillary processing expenses (e.g., mailing, administrative overhead).
  • These generally do not vary by age or policy size but are incurred for each applicant tested.

7. Decision Criteria

  1. Break-Even Method:

    • Find the coverage amount (or threshold) at which the present value of M/M savings equals the underwriting procedure’s cost.
    • The interest rate is chosen to reflect the insurer’s opportunity cost or internal rate for reserves.

  2. Return on Investment (ROI) Method:

    • Solve for the discount rate at which the present value of savings equals the cost for a given amount of insurance.
    • If the resulting ROI is higher than the insurer’s internal target rate, the procedure is deemed to have protective value at that coverage level.
  • These two methods produce consistent conclusions if the same discount rate is used as the insurer’s time-value of money. However, the break-even method emphasizes an “amount of insurance” threshold, whereas ROI focuses on the implied rate of return.

Conclusion

The paper provides a simple and general approach to determine the protective value of underwriting procedures. By breaking down:

  • How to measure excess mortality/morbidity,
  • The significance of persistency and prevalence,
  • The time-value of money (interest rate and time horizon),
  • And the costs involved,

the author shows how insurers can systematically quantify whether the savings from identifying substandard risks outweigh the expenses of underwriting. This framework is then applied (in hypothetical examples) to illustrate how to decide if an underwriting method is economically justified, either by finding the break-even coverage threshold or by comparing the ROI to the insurer’s required rate of return.
