GEO

Abstract

The emergence of large language models (LLMs) has transformed search engines, leading to the creation of “generative engines” (GEs) that use these models to directly generate and summarize responses to user queries. Unlike traditional search engines that rely on showing links to external websites, generative engines compile and synthesize information from multiple sources to provide a cohesive answer. This shift has made search engines more useful to users, offering faster, often more personalized responses, but it also presents a challenge to content creators, whose work may be used by these engines without much transparency or control over how it appears in the responses.

To support content creators within this new system, a new approach called Generative Engine Optimization (GEO) has been introduced. GEO offers a framework to help content creators improve the chances that their content will be featured in generative engine responses, despite the black-box nature of these models. This optimization strategy allows creators to focus on increasing the visibility of their content through customizable visibility metrics, despite limited direct control over generative engine behavior.

A benchmark, GEO-bench, has been developed to evaluate the effectiveness of GEO. GEO-bench includes a large set of diverse user queries spanning multiple topics and sources, allowing a structured way to assess visibility improvements. Initial evaluations show that GEO can enhance content visibility in generative engine responses by as much as 40%. Additionally, GEO’s effectiveness varies across different types of content, suggesting that tailored strategies are needed for different domains.

This research highlights a new direction in the development of search technology and information accessibility, with significant implications for both the creators of generative engines and content providers.

Introduction

Traditional search engines, invented around 30 years ago, transformed global information access by providing lists of relevant websites in response to user queries. However, these engines were limited in that they only pointed users toward information rather than providing answers directly. The rise of large language models (LLMs), such as those in tools like BingChat, Google’s Search Generative Experience (SGE), and perplexity.ai, has now led to the development of “generative engines” (GEs). These new systems blend traditional search with generative models to gather and synthesize information from multiple sources, offering comprehensive, multi-faceted responses rather than just lists of links.

Generative engines bring significant benefits to users by delivering information faster and more accurately and to developers by improving engagement and satisfaction. However, they present a challenge for content creators. Unlike traditional search engines, generative engines reduce users’ need to visit websites, potentially lowering organic traffic and impacting creators who rely on site visits for revenue and visibility. Additionally, the proprietary and opaque nature of GEs leaves content creators with little insight or control over how their content is used or displayed.

To address these challenges, the authors introduce Generative Engine Optimization (GEO), a novel framework designed to help content creators adapt their content for generative engines. GEO uses a flexible, black-box optimization approach to improve the visibility of web content in generative responses. By adjusting content presentation, style, and structure, GEO aims to enhance a website’s chances of being cited or included in generative responses.

One of GEO’s key innovations is its visibility metrics, which go beyond traditional rankings to measure visibility across dimensions like citation placement, relevance, and influence within the response. Since generative engines use complex, structured outputs, these new metrics are necessary for assessing visibility more accurately than traditional search engines, which simply rank results linearly.

To test GEO’s effectiveness, the authors developed GEO-bench, a large benchmark containing 10,000 queries across various domains. Evaluations of GEO’s methods show visibility improvements of up to 40% across different queries, with strategies like including citations, quotations, and relevant statistics significantly boosting a source’s presence in generative responses. Real-world tests on platforms like Perplexity.ai confirmed these gains, demonstrating a 37% improvement in content visibility.

In summary, the contributions of this work are:

  1. Generative Engine Optimization (GEO): The first optimization framework for improving content visibility in generative engines, with up to a 40% boost in some cases.
  2. Customized Visibility Metrics: GEO introduces a new set of visibility metrics tailored to generative engine requirements, allowing creators to optimize content with precision.
  3. GEO-bench Benchmark: The first large-scale, diverse benchmark for evaluating content optimization techniques specifically for generative engines, supporting rigorous testing and improvement.

This work provides an essential toolset for content creators adapting to the generative search landscape and marks an advancement in optimizing for information discovery in the era of generative AI.

Formulation and Methodology

2.1 Formulation of Generative Engines (GEs):

Generative engines (GEs) are advanced search systems that combine traditional search functions with generative language models. They work by retrieving relevant documents from the web (or a database) and using AI models to generate a summarized response, complete with inline citations. Unlike traditional search engines, which display a ranked list of websites, GEs synthesize content from multiple sources into a structured answer. This structure presents challenges in defining and measuring visibility (or “impression”) because GEs do not just list results but instead embed citations within a response, intermixing various sources.

A typical GE workflow involves:

  1. Breaking down the user’s query into smaller sub-queries.
  2. Sending these to a search engine to retrieve sources.
  3. Summarizing each source.
  4. Combining summaries into a cohesive response.

This process is illustrated by a setup resembling BingChat, which splits a complex query into simpler parts, retrieves relevant sources, summarizes them, and integrates these summaries into a structured response. Since GEs aim to be accurate and avoid misinformation, every fact in the response ideally links to a supporting source.

2.2 Generative Engine Optimization (GEO):

Generative Engine Optimization (GEO) is proposed as a new framework to help content creators increase their visibility within GE responses. Traditional SEO strategies aren’t effective with GEs because GEs generate responses based on a more nuanced understanding of language and meaning rather than simple keyword matching. GEO is designed to maximize a website’s impression by ensuring it is frequently and prominently cited within responses. This visibility depends on relevance to the query and placement within the answer.

2.2.1 Defining Impressions for Generative Engines:

Since visibility metrics from traditional search engines don’t fit well with GEs, GEO introduces new “impression” metrics tailored to these systems:

  1. Word Count Impression: Measures the prominence of a source by the number of words from a citation in the response. A higher word count indicates greater prominence.
  2. Position-Adjusted Word Count: Weights citations more heavily if they appear earlier in the response since users are likelier to read the beginning of an answer.
  3. Subjective Impression Metric: Considers subjective factors like relevance, citation influence, and material uniqueness. It uses a tool called G-Eval to evaluate the quality of these subjective metrics.

2.2.2 GEO Methods for Websites:

To optimize content for GE visibility, GEO offers several strategies that help improve how often and prominently a website is cited:

  1. Authoritative Style: Makes text sound more convincing.
  2. Statistics Addition: Adds quantitative data for clarity.
  3. Keyword Inclusion: Incorporates query-relevant keywords.
  4. Citing Sources and Adding Quotations: Increases credibility by citing trusted sources.
  5. Easy-to-Understand Language: Simplifies complex language for better comprehension.
  6. Fluency Optimization: Ensures text flows naturally.
  7. Unique Terms: Uses specific or technical terms to stand out.

Most methods enhance existing content presentation, while a few (like adding sources and quotations) may require additional information.

Testing GEO Methods:

The effectiveness of each method is tested by applying them individually to website content in response to a query and evaluating the improvements in visibility metrics. Each GEO method, therefore, aims to make website content more compatible with generative engine requirements, improving the chances of being cited within AI-generated responses.

In essence, GEO offers a range of techniques for content creators to boost visibility in a world where generative engines, not traditional search engines, may increasingly control the flow of information.

Experimental Setup

Summary and Explanation:

3.1 Evaluated Generative Engine: This experimental setup mimics common commercial generative engines (like You.com and Perplexity.ai) by using a two-step process:

  1. Retrieving the top 5 most relevant sources from Google for each query.
  2. Using an AI language model (specifically GPT-3.5 Turbo) to generate responses based on these sources.

The use of only the top 5 sources helps manage limitations in processing power and cost when working with large language models. Each query receives multiple generated responses, reducing variability in the results.

3.2 Benchmark: GEO-bench To evaluate the effectiveness of Generative Engine Optimization (GEO) methods, the GEO-bench dataset was created. This benchmark includes 10,000 diverse queries, categorized by topic, complexity, and user intent, providing a robust test environment for assessing GEO methods.

The queries in GEO-bench are drawn from nine sources:

  • MS Macro, ORCAS-1, Natural Questions: Represent general user queries from search engines.
  • All Souls, LIMA: Include complex questions requiring reasoning and aggregation of information.
  • Davinci-Debate: Questions designed to test debate and reasoning skills.
  • Perplexity.ai Discover, ELI-5: Complex questions from Perplexity.ai and Reddit that require simplified explanations.
  • GPT-4 Generated Queries: A diverse set of synthetic queries across different domains.

Each query in GEO-bench is associated with the top 5 Google search results, allowing for a thorough test of how well GEO methods enhance content visibility.

Tags in GEO-bench: Each query is tagged by category (like Arts, Health, Games), user intent (informational, transactional, navigational), and complexity. This tagging helps content creators select the right GEO method based on the nature of the query, allowing for more targeted optimization.

3.3 GEO Methods Nine GEO methods are tested, each aimed at improving different aspects of content visibility within generative engine responses. These methods include tactics like adding authoritative language, including statistics, simplifying language, and quoting sources. Each method’s effectiveness is compared to a baseline (unmodified content), and results are averaged to minimize randomness.

3.4 Evaluation Metrics Two main metrics are used to assess GEO method performance:

  1. Position-Adjusted Word Count: Measures visibility based on both word count and citation position, giving greater weight to sources that appear earlier in the response.
  2. Subjective Impression: Measures a source’s relevance and influence within the response, as well as its uniqueness, diversity, position, and click likelihood. This is further broken into sub-metrics to capture different factors that may impact user attention and engagement.

To standardize these metrics, they are normalized so that all citation impressions in a response add up to 1. The relative improvement in visibility is calculated by comparing the baseline impression with the improved impression after applying GEO methods.

Performance Results The study shows that traditional SEO techniques, like keyword stuffing, perform poorly in generative engines. In contrast, methods such as Quotation Addition and Statistics Addition yield substantial improvements, increasing visibility by up to 41% in Position-Adjusted Word Count and by 28% in Subjective Impression. This indicates that content enhancements focusing on authoritative information and direct citations are more effective for improving visibility within generative engines.

Results

Summary and Explanation:

4. Results Overview This study evaluated various Generative Engine Optimization (GEO) methods designed to improve website visibility in Generative Engine (GE) responses. By testing these methods on GEO-bench, a diverse set of user queries, the researchers measured performance using two metrics:

  1. Position-Adjusted Word Count: Considers both the word count and the citation’s position in a GE’s response.
  2. Subjective Impression: Combines several subjective factors like relevance, credibility, and uniqueness to create an overall impression score.

Key Findings The results indicate that GEO methods consistently outperform the baseline (no optimization) across these metrics, demonstrating their robustness across various types of queries. Specifically:

  • Top-performing methods: The “Cite Sources,” “Quotation Addition,” and “Statistics Addition” methods yielded the highest visibility gains. These methods, which involve adding relevant statistics, credible quotes, and reliable citations, achieved:
    • 30-40% improvement on the Position-Adjusted Word Count metric.
    • 15-30% improvement on the Subjective Impression metric.
    • These techniques enhance both the content’s credibility and the richness of the information, requiring only minor adjustments to existing content.

Effectiveness Based on Search Rank The study found that GEO methods are especially helpful for content from lower-ranked websites in traditional search results. For instance, when using “Cite Sources” and “Quotation Addition” methods, visibility increased significantly for content that wasn’t initially highly ranked in search engines, thus providing a path for less prominent websites to gain visibility in GE responses.

Top-performing Tags by GEO Method Each GEO method performs well in specific content categories:

  • Authoritative Style: Effective for categories like Debate, History, and Science.
  • Fluency Optimization: Beneficial in Business, Science, and Health.
  • Cite Sources: Works best in fact-based or legal content.
  • Quotation Addition: Effective in People & Society, Explanation, and History.
  • Statistics Addition: Valuable in Law & Government, Debate, and Opinion content.

These findings suggest that content creators can tailor their optimization strategies based on their website’s target domain for maximum effectiveness.

Stylistic Changes and Presentation Improving readability and fluency in the text (Fluency Optimization and Easy-to-Understand methods) also boosted visibility by 15-30%, highlighting that GE responses value not just factual content but also how well information is presented. This suggests that GEs prioritize content that is clear, well-organized, and easy to understand.

Keyword Stuffing and Authoritative Tone Traditional SEO tactics like keyword stuffing proved ineffective in GEs, offering little to no improvement in visibility. Additionally, adding a more persuasive or authoritative tone did not significantly boost visibility, indicating that GEs already have built-in mechanisms to assess content quality without requiring overt stylistic changes.

Conclusion

These results show that optimization strategies effective for traditional search engines may not work as well in Generative Engines. Instead, focusing on content credibility, presentation, and adding authoritative sources or statistics are more effective ways to increase visibility. For content creators, this suggests a shift from traditional SEO techniques to those that align more closely with the nuanced evaluation methods of GEs, helping ensure their content remains visible and valuable in this new search paradigm.

Analysis

Summary and Explanation:

5.1 Domain-Specific Generative Engine Optimizations This section highlights that certain Generative Engine Optimization (GEO) methods are more effective in specific content domains:

  • Authoritative Tone: Effective for debate-style and historical content, where a persuasive writing style can enhance credibility.
  • Cite Sources: Particularly useful for factual content, as citations validate the facts and improve the credibility of the response.
  • Statistics Addition: Works best in fields like Law & Government and Opinion content, where data-driven evidence can increase the authority and visibility of the content.
  • Quotation Addition: Most effective in domains like People & Society, Explanation, and History, as quotes add authenticity and depth, enhancing visibility.

This analysis suggests that website owners should make targeted, domain-specific optimizations to improve their visibility on generative engines.

5.2 Optimization of Multiple Websites The study also examined how GEO methods affect the visibility of websites across different search rankings:

  • Lower-ranked websites benefit more from GEO because they are typically disadvantaged in traditional search rankings due to factors like limited backlinks. GEO can improve their visibility in Generative Engine responses, offering them a fairer playing field.
  • For instance, using “Cite Sources” increased the visibility of fifth-ranked websites by 115.1%, while top-ranked websites saw a decrease in visibility by 30.3% when GEO methods were applied across all content sources.

This shows that GEO methods could help democratize online visibility, offering smaller websites a chance to compete effectively against larger ones in Generative Engine responses.

5.3 Combination of GEO Strategies Combining GEO strategies generally yields better results than using them individually. The analysis of strategy combinations showed that:

  • Fluency Optimization and Statistics Addition together produced the highest visibility improvements, outperforming individual strategies by over 5.5%.
  • Cite Sources also performed well in combination with other strategies, boosting visibility significantly, even though it was less effective alone.

This suggests that content creators can achieve optimal results by combining multiple GEO methods tailored to their content.

5.4 Qualitative Analysis Examples in this section illustrate how GEO methods improve visibility with minimal changes:

  • Adding a citation source significantly increases visibility, as seen in a query about Swiss chocolate, where citing a study on chocolate consumption boosted the content’s credibility.
  • Incorporating relevant statistics, like mentioning a 70% increase in robotic involvement, makes content more authoritative and informative.
  • Enhancing the tone (e.g., using a more persuasive or impactful style) also improves visibility, especially in responses that require a clear stance or opinion.

These examples show that even minor adjustments, such as citing credible sources or adding statistics, can substantially improve visibility in Generative Engine responses without extensive content changes.

Conclusion

This analysis highlights the importance of domain-specific and combined GEO strategies in improving visibility on Generative Engines. Small content creators and lower-ranked websites can especially benefit, making GEO an effective approach to leveling the digital playing field. Content creators are encouraged to experiment with multiple GEO strategies and tailor them to their content domains for the best results in Generative Engine responses.

Summary and Explanation

6. GEO in the Wild: Testing on Perplexity.ai The GEO methods were tested on Perplexity.ai, a widely-used generative engine, to confirm their real-world effectiveness. Key findings include:

  • Quotation Addition improved visibility the most, with a 22% increase in Position-Adjusted Word Count over the baseline, indicating that citing quotes from credible sources is particularly effective.
  • Statistics Addition also boosted visibility by 37%, while traditional SEO methods like Keyword Stuffing underperformed, showing 10% less effectiveness than the baseline.

These results underscore three main points:

  1. The effectiveness of domain-specific GEO methods in real-world applications.
  2. The adaptability of GEO methods across various generative engines.
  3. The practicality of GEO methods for content creators, as they can easily implement them to improve visibility.

7. Related Work This work builds on previous research in evidence-backed answer generation, where models use sources to generate responses. While past methods focused on fetching sources or manipulating model outputs, GEO introduces non-adversarial strategies that optimize content for generative engines.

Additionally, GEO differs from traditional Search Engine Optimization (SEO) as it involves optimizing for generative models rather than keyword-based ranking. GEO considers multi-modal outputs, conversational structures, and varied query reformulation, necessitating different strategies than standard SEO.

8. Conclusion This research introduces the concept of Generative Engine Optimization (GEO), which allows content creators to enhance their content’s visibility in generative engine results. Key contributions include:

  • GEO-bench: A benchmark dataset of diverse queries with sources for evaluating GEO methods.
  • Effective GEO Methods: Techniques like adding citations, statistics, and quotations were shown to increase visibility by up to 40%.
  • Commercial Application: Testing on a real generative engine (Perplexity.ai) demonstrated the high real-world impact of GEO methods, especially for small creators and lower-ranked sites, by making visibility improvements accessible without relying on traditional SEO factors like backlinks.

The research formalizes GEO as a new paradigm, offering tools, benchmarks, and methods to enhance generative engine optimization across various content domains.

9. Limitations While promising, GEO has certain limitations:

  1. Adaptability: GEO methods might need to evolve as generative engines change, similar to SEO’s ongoing adaptation.
  2. Query Evolution: GEO-bench may require updates to stay aligned with shifting real-world query patterns.
  3. Impact on Search Rankings: Since GEO focuses on text optimization rather than metadata like domain or backlinks, it’s unclear how it affects traditional search rankings, though it likely has minimal impact.
  4. Context Limitations: Larger context lengths in future models could allow them to process more sources, potentially diminishing the emphasis on high search rankings.

In sum, GEO represents an innovative step toward helping content creators adapt to the generative search engine landscape, with practical implications for enhancing visibility across multiple platforms and evolving search paradigms.
