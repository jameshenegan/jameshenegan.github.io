A “wicked problem” refers to a complex issue that is difficult or impossible to solve due to its multifaceted, interconnected nature and the lack of a clear, definitive solution. The term was introduced in 1973 by design theorists Horst Rittel and Melvin Webber in the context of social policy and planning, where traditional problem-solving methods often fall short[1][2].

Key Characteristics of Wicked Problems

Rittel and Webber outlined several defining features of wicked problems:

  1. No Definitive Formulation: The problem cannot be clearly or fully defined.
  2. No Stopping Rule: There is no clear point at which the problem can be considered solved.
  3. Solutions Are Not Right or Wrong: Instead, they are judged as better or worse based on context.
  4. Irreversible Solutions: Each attempt to solve the problem has significant consequences, making trial-and-error approaches impractical.
  5. Unique Nature: Every wicked problem is essentially one-of-a-kind.
  6. Interconnectedness: Solving one aspect of the problem often creates or reveals new issues.
  7. Stakeholder Disagreement: Different stakeholders may have conflicting views on the nature of the problem and its solutions.
  8. Dynamic Constraints: The resources, constraints, and conditions affecting the problem evolve over time[1][2][3].

Examples of Wicked Problems

Wicked problems are commonly found in areas such as:

  • Climate Change: Involves global cooperation, regional impacts, and evolving science.
  • Poverty: Intersects with education, healthcare, and economic systems.
  • Healthcare Reform: Balances cost, accessibility, and quality across diverse populations.
  • Homelessness: Combines social, economic, and policy challenges[1][3][5].

Why Are They “Wicked”?

The term “wicked” does not imply evil but rather resistance to resolution. These problems often involve deep social complexity, where every proposed solution affects numerous interconnected factors. Unlike “tame” problems (e.g., mathematical puzzles), wicked problems defy standard methods of resolution and require innovative, collaborative approaches[1][6].

In summary, wicked problems are characterized by their complexity, lack of definitive solutions, and evolving nature. Addressing them requires adaptive thinking, stakeholder collaboration, and a focus on mitigating rather than fully resolving issues.
