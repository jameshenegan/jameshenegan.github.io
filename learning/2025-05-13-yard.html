Here are four evergreen, wet-tolerant shrubs that thrive in central-Mississippi (Zone 8) shade/part-sun and quickly knit into a living screen. Any one of them can slot straight into the gaps where your three ligustrum now stand—or you can mix two for texture.

# Shrub & Size (10 yr) Why it fits your low, sometimes-soggy spot Look / bonus features Planting notes*
1 Southern Wax Myrtle Morella cerifera (10-15 ft H × 8-12 ft W)** Native; shrugs off periodic flooding and clay, but also handles summer drought. Fastest grower here—expect 2-3 ft a year once established.(Mississippi State Extension Service, LSU AgCenter) Dense olive-green foliage year-round; tiny fragrant leaves; blue-gray berries for birds. Can be sheared for a formal hedge or limbed up into multi-trunk “screens.” Space 5-6 ft apart from sweet olives. Prune after spring growth flush to keep width in check.
2 Florida/Yellow Anise Illicium parviflorum ‘Forest Green’ or ‘BananAppeal’ (8-12 ft H × 6-8 ft W)** Grows where few evergreens will—moist, even boggy, and only 2 hrs of direct sun. Aromatic leaves deter deer.(NC Extension Gardener Toolbox, Gardenia) Glossy chartreuse-to-deep-green leaves; star-anise-scent when crushed; tiny spring blooms. Naturally full to the ground—no “leggy” base. Space 4-5 ft apart. Minimal pruning—just tip after bloom if you need to keep height under sweet olives.
3 Walter’s Viburnum Viburnum obovatum ‘Whorled Class’ or ‘Densa’ (10-12 ft H × 6-8 ft W)** Evergreen in Zone 8; handles wet feet and part shade. Wildlife magnet.(gardeningsolutions.ifas.ufl.edu, Florida Wildflower Foundation) Masses of white flowers in March-April, glossy foliage, red-black berries. Space 4-6 ft. Shear lightly after bloom to maintain wall-of-green effect.
4 Columnar Yaupon Holly Ilex vomitoria ‘Will Fleming’ (15 ft H × 3-4 ft W)** Thrives in both occasional flood and dry spells; drought, heat, even brief freezes.(Natural Resources Conservation Service, NC Extension Gardener Toolbox) Tall, pencil-slim pillars that give instant vertical screen without stealing lawn. Female forms (‘Scarlett’s Peak’) add red berries. Plant 3-4 ft off fence, 4 ft apart. Clip once a year for tidy columns or let them plume naturally.

*All appreciate slightly acid soil—work 2–3 in. of pine bark fines or leaf-mold into the planting back-fill and top-dress yearly with pine-straw mulch.

Design tweaks to boost “lush”

  1. Create a gentle berm (6-8 in.) against the fence with native topsoil + compost. It lifts roots out of the worst puddling yet still lets moisture wick up when dry.
  2. Layer in moisture-loving perennials at the front: Louisiana iris, swamp milkweed, or purple pickerel-rush add color and help absorb excess water.
  3. Thread an evergreen vine along the fence for instant coverage while shrubs grow—Confederate jasmine or native Carolina jessamine can root right into the berm and won’t mind your midday sun window.
  4. Stagger heights: if you keep both sweet olives and the new screen shrubs, offset them slightly instead of a straight line; that 3-D layering makes foliage overlap sooner and hides fence pickets faster.

Plant-by-plant quick plan

  • Fastest full fence-cover: 3 × Wax Myrtle (back row) + jasmine vine (front of fence).
  • Most shade-adapted & deer-proof: 3 × Florida Anise, mulch heavily, no vine needed.
  • Flower & berry interest: Alternate Walter’s Viburnum and Yaupon Hollies (female), 5 plants total, for a tapestry of white bloom, red berries, and evergreen leaves.

Give any of these choices one Mississippi growing season and you’ll start seeing green between every picket; by year three the wood should disappear behind foliage almost year-round.
